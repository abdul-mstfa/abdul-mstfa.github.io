Information Rate

March 30, 2024

Think of information as being supplied at different frequency levels. These frequency levels define how quickly new information arises or how quickly old information is updated.

    1. Low frequency: this is where things like mathematics, physics, computer science, and evolutionary theory exist - to name a few. This is the far left side of the spectrum. The update rate is so low here that it is a reliable substrate upon which to build. Imagine if the "laws of physics" were being updated every hour, can reliable engineering (i.e. applied science) exist?

    2. High frequency: at the other end of the spectrum we get things like news and social media. With every refresh you are supplied with an endless barrage of content.

In the low frequency domain, the signal is fairly clean as the information goes through a rigorous noise filtering mechanism. The incentive is to produce robust, well researched, and heavily tested & filtered content. Whereas in the high frequency domain, the signal is often quite noisy. The incentive is speed and attention. Information exists along a sliding scale of frequencies. Think of breaking news versus the long news cycles (eg. Israel/Palestine conflict, the Cold War, China's uprising and risks to the US). Not all news are super high frequency, but most tend to be.

If you think back to the last 4 years (this writing is in March, 2024), which news items have stuck and have had/will likely have measurable impact on today's society? I can name but a few: * The Covid-19 pandemic and all associated consequences * Russia/Ukraine Conflict * China's increased posturing as it pertains to Taiwan's sovereignty * AI's escape velocity out of research hubs and proliferation into personal and commercial use cases

These examples illustrate the difference between transient noise and enduring high-frequency events that transition into longer-term conversation. They remain at the forefront of global awareness because their impact is significant, ongoing, and measurable. Whether discussing low-frequency sciences or high-frequency media, understanding the pace at which knowledge evolves helps us evaluate its reliability and significance. A balanced information diet—recognizing when we’re dealing with tried-and-true fundamentals versus quickly shifting headlines—can help us become more discerning consumers, producers, and sharers of knowledge.
